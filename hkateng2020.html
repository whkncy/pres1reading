How to Be a Good Speaker

If the thought of speaking in front of the class gives you cold sweats, you are not alone[cite: 1]. Learning certain speaking skills can help you gain confidence and make an impressive presentation[cite: 1]. Here are some tips for you[cite: 1].

(i) Everyone knows the importance of having interesting ideas in a presentation[cite: 1]. However, there are other essential things[cite: 1]. According to Roy M. Berko, an expert in communication, only 7% of the meaning comes from the words used[cite: 1]. 93% of the meaning of what people say comes from non-verbal signals, such as facial expressions and body language[cite: 1]. How to use your voice is also important[cite: 1]. The emotions in your voice have to match what you are saying[cite: 1]. An appropriate tone and style should also be used during your presentation[cite: 1].

(ii) While giving a presentation, most speakers find it difficult to control their speed, especially when they are nervous[cite: 1]. You may think that speaking rapidly like lightning would help you relax[cite: 1]. However, it does not help you give a good presentation[cite: 1]. When you deliver a speech in a rush, it is hard for the audience to follow, and they might get tired of listening to you[cite: 1]. Why not slow down to let your words be heard more clearly and to be better understood?[cite: 1]

(iii) You should also pay extra attention to how you break down a sentence into parts by taking pauses whenever needed[cite: 1]. For example, when you say 'Let's eat, Grandpa', you have to pause after 'eat' so that the sentence sounds like a friendly invitation[cite: 1]. Otherwise, people may think that you are asking others to 'eat Grandpa' with you![cite: 1] Pausing correctly can save you from an embarrassing situation[cite: 1].

(iv) To conclude, your non-verbal signals, tone, speed and pauses all contribute to how well your audience understands your presentation[cite: 1]. Practise more and you will be able to give an impressive presentation confidently next time![cite: 1]

An Afternoon at the Community Centre

Yesterday afternoon, Tom visited the Shatin Community Centre to see his cousin, Chris, who was participating in a charity talent show. When Tom arrived at 2:00 p.m., the main hall was already packed with families, and energetic music was playing loudly from the stage speakers.

The event kicked off with a vibrant traditional lion dance that immediately captured everyone's attention. Following that, a group of primary school children performed a rhythmic skipping dance. Chris went on stage third to play a classical piece on his violin. Although Chris looked slightly nervous at the beginning, he played beautifully and received loud cheers from the crowd.

After the performances concluded at 4:00 p.m., Tom and Chris walked out to the outdoor courtyard. The centre had organized several fun carnival stalls to raise funds for a local animal shelter. Chris wanted to try the game stalls while Tom was drawn to a handicraft booth where volunteers were teaching people how to make traditional paper lanterns. They spent another hour enjoying the activities before heading home together.
